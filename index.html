Top ten biggest hacks in History

  • Yahoo data breach (2013–2016)
      A series of attacks that ultimately compromised ~3 billion user accounts — the largest known breach of a U.S.-linked internet company. Affected Yahoo email accounts and related services (Tumblr, Flickr, etc.).
  • Equifax Data Breach (2017)
      Breach at major U.S. credit bureau exposed sensitive personal info (Social Security numbers, birthdates, etc.) of about 147.9 million Americans — arguably one of the worst identity-theft enabling hacks in U.S. history.
  • 2014 JPMorgan Chase data breach
      A massive breach that affected data tied to roughly 83 million accounts — households & small businesses — showing that even top-tier banks are vulnerable.
  • Office of Personnel Management data breach (OPM breach, 2015)
      Attack on U.S. government personnel-records agency exposed sensitive security-clearance data — hundreds of thousands to millions of personnel files compromised, long-term national-security implications.
  • Moonlight Maze (1996–1999)
      One of the earliest and most serious government-espionage hacks: attackers infiltrated multiple U.S. agencies (defense, DOE, academia, etc.), reportedly extracting deep secrets over years.
  • Colonial Pipeline ransomware attack (2021)
      Hack on major U.S. pipeline infrastructure forced shutdown of a vital oil supply route — showing cyberattacks can have real-world, national-scale physical consequences beyond data theft.
  • Major telecom networks hack (2024 global telecommunications hack)
      Attack reportedly breached major U.S. internet/telecom providers, giving hackers access to metadata (call logs, texts) of millions — a huge privacy and national-security issue.
  • Large-scale corporate data breaches (e.g. social networks, media) — e.g. hacking of social networks / media companies
      Between 2013–2019 many U.S. companies were breached, exposing vast numbers of accounts: social media, media, hospitality, etc.
  • Repeated cyclical “data-dump” hacks combining many smaller/medium breaches
      Because many breaches accumulate over time (some public, some private), the aggregate impact — identity theft, credential stuffing, privacy violations — becomes enormous.
  • Cyber-espionage and supply-chain attacks (e.g. affecting government, defense, contractors)
      Beyond single events, sustained campaigns targeting infrastructure and government agencies (sometimes over years) have undermined trust in digital security and exposed systemic vulnerabilities.
